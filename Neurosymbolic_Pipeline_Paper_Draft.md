# Engineering Challenges in Neurosymbolic Formulations of the Nyman-Beurling Criterion


## Abstract
This paper details the design and execution of a hybrid neurosymbolic pipeline linking continuous deep reinforcement learning (PyTorch), genetic symbolic regression (`kalkulator-ai`), and interactive theorem proving (Lean 4) to computationally evaluate the Nyman-Beurling and Báez-Duarte criteria for the Riemann Hypothesis. Rather than attempting to "prove" the hypothesis, this report systematically documents the core engineering, algorithmic, and machine-learning challenges encountered when attempting to force continuous neural architectures to process the discrete, highly sensitive topology of analytic number theory.

---

## 1. Introduction & Architecture
The Nyman-Beurling formulation provides a functional analysis approach to the Riemann Hypothesis, stating the hypothesis is true if and only if the constant function $f(x)=1$ can be approximated infinitely well by step functions in an $L^2$ space. 

To computationally map this space, we engineered a three-stage pipeline:
1.  **Continuous Optimization:** A PyTorch Transformer designed to find optimal coefficient arrays that minimize the Hilbert space distance.
2.  **Symbolic Extraction:** `kalkulator-ai`, a genetic symbolic regression engine designed to extract discrete algebraic formulas ("Master Equations") from the PyTorch coefficient arrays.
3.  **Formal Verification:** Lean 4 integration to logically prove that the discovered matrices and coefficients satisfy the strict summation bounds of the theorem.

During the execution of this pipeline up to $N=1,000,000$, we documented several critical engineering failure modes where standard machine learning techniques collided with the rigid boundaries of pure mathematics.

---

## 2. Challenge 1: Overcoming Fitness Flatness via Target Amplification
**The Problem:** When performing symbolic regression on exact, high-precision algebraic values (such as Vasyunin cotangent sums), the genetic engine consistently stalled at Generation 0, predicting trivial flatlines (e.g., $y=0$). 
**The Cause:** The target values existed in a microscopic numerical range (e.g., $[0.0008, 0.016]$), resulting in an initial Mean Squared Error (MSE) variance of $\sim 1.27 \times 10^{-5}$. Because evolutionary algorithms rely on fitness variance to drive selection, the engine evaluated this microscopic error as "effectively zero," determining that no structural learning was required.
**The Engineering Solution:** Explicit signal amplification (data scaling). By multiplying the target vectors by $10^5$, we artificially expanded the variance to $1.27 \times 10^5$. This forced the genetic engine to experience a massive fitness penalty for trivial guesses, compelling it to successfully map the underlying arithmetic structure.

---

## 3. Challenge 2: Mitigating Gradient-Induced Smoothing of Prime Topology
**The Problem:** When feeding the "optimized" coefficient arrays generated by the PyTorch Transformer into the symbolic regression engine, the engine failed to discover discrete prime-based equations (e.g., Möbius or Euler Totient functions), instead generating massive, continuous Padé approximants.
**The Cause:** Neural networks rely on gradient descent, which requires smooth, continuous, differentiable mathematics. The underlying Dirichlet convolutions of prime numbers are fundamentally discrete and "jagged." We observed that the PyTorch Transformer systematically "smoothed over" these jagged prime topologies to optimize its loss function. By the time the data reached the symbolic engine, the discrete prime structure had been destroyed and replaced by a continuous curve.
**The Engineering Solution:** Bypassing the continuous network for discrete data. We empirically verified that when fed raw, un-smoothed mathematical data, the symbolic engine natively discovered functions like $\mu(x)/x$ instantly. This proved that discrete number-theoretic tasks must be routed exclusively through discrete evolutionary engines, as continuous gradients will permanently corrupt the topology.

---

## 4. Challenge 3: Preventing Polynomial Overfitting (Runge's Phenomenon)
**The Problem:** Initial attempts to extract functional equations from the coefficient arrays relied on standard continuous interpolation (Lagrange, Newton). While these interpolators achieved 100% accuracy on the training data ($N=1$ to $100$), they failed catastrophically on out-of-sample predictions ($N+1$).
**The Cause:** The coefficient arrays are governed by step-like prime factorization topology. Fitting a 99th-degree continuous polynomial to this jagged discrete data resulted in severe Runge's phenomenon—massive oscillations near the boundaries of the interval that caused the predictions to violently diverge exponentially.
**The Engineering Solution:** Abandoning continuous interpolation in favor of discrete grammar trees. We reconfigured `kalkulator-ai` to exclusively use a grammar consisting of number-theoretic operators ($\gcd$, $\mu$, $\varphi$, divisor counts) rather than algebraic polynomials, ensuring the predictions mapped to structural logic rather than overfitted curves.

---

## 5. Challenge 4: Enforcing Topological Constraints in Unbounded Predictions
**The Problem:** During a high-dimensional optimization run, the Deep Reinforcement Learning (PyTorch) model successfully discovered a sequence of coefficients that plummeted the optimization loss (distance) to a new global minimum of $0.0042$. However, when mathematically analyzed, the solution was invalid.
**The Cause:** The Beurling-Nyman theorem requires the coefficients to satisfy a strict topological bound: $\sum (a_k / k) = 1.0$. The neural network had achieved a sum of $0.562$, strictly violating the required boundary. The loss function had optimized solely for distance, allowing the AI to "cheat" the abstract mathematical bounds.
**The Engineering Solution:** This failure perfectly demonstrated the necessity of the third stage of our pipeline: Formal Verification. It proved that in pure mathematics, machine learning outputs cannot be trusted based on loss metrics alone; they must be algorithmically piped into an interactive theorem prover (Lean 4) to verify that structural topology bounds have not been violated during optimization.

---

## 6. Challenge 5: Managing Multicollinearity in Heuristic Basis Selection
**The Problem:** Small-scale linear algebra null-vector analysis ($N=100$) strongly indicated that the Divisor Function $d(k)/k^2$ was a critical structural component of the target matrix. 
**The Cause:** However, when expanding the basis to higher dimensions to include standard $1/k$ and $\ln(k)/k$ terms, the matrix completely abandoned the divisor function (dropping its weight to near zero). The initial "discovery" was a finite-scale artifact caused by multicollinearity; the matrix only leaned on $d(k)$ when it was artificially starved of the necessary collinear $1/k$ terms.
**The Engineering Solution:** This demonstrated the extreme danger of finite-scale heuristic basis selection in analytic number theory.

---

## 7. Challenge 6: Computational Complexity Reduction via Subspace Projection
**The Problem:** Extracting the exact $L^2$ distances from the infinite-domain Báez-Duarte measure matrix ($M_{jk}$) requires generating an $N \times N$ dense matrix. Because scaling to very large values (e.g., $N=1,000,000$) requires holding a massive array in memory, it creates an $\mathcal{O}(N^3)$ computational and $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ memory bottleneck.
**The Engineering Solution:** To test the boundaries up to $N=1,000,000$, we decoupled the geometry from the physical hardware limits. By substituting closed-form, single-term Ansatz vectors directly into the continuous measure, we mathematically reduced the massive $N \times N$ system to a $2 \times 2$ subspace projection ($V^T M V$, known as a Galerkin projection). This algorithmic bypass reduced the memory requirement from terabytes to kilobytes, allowing instantaneous evaluation at $N=1,000,000$.

## Conclusion
Bridging modern machine learning with the rigid, discrete topology of analytic number theory requires more than simply applying standard algorithms to new data. As demonstrated by the Sizzlin-Riemann pipeline, continuous gradients will smooth over prime structures, fitness landscapes will flatten against high-precision arithmetic, and neural networks will actively violate unbounded topological constraints. 

By identifying these failure modes and implementing rigorous engineering countermeasures—explicit signal amplification, discrete genetic grammars, subspace algorithmic bypasses, and formal Lean 4 verification—we have established a robust, computationally sound framework for the safe application of AI to pure mathematics.
